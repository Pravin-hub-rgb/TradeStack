## Q4: As we are doing swing trading which also involves BTST trades, just wanted to know what will be the implications if we have a short delivery? Have you ever come across any situation where Short Delivery penalty has been charged for selling in T+1 or T+2 day?

### Answer
Short delivery in BTST (Buy Today Sell Tomorrow) trading occurs when your broker fails to deliver shares to your account by the settlement date (T+2). This can happen when the seller's broker doesn't receive the shares in time, creating a chain reaction where you also face penalties for selling shares you never received. However, this is an extremely rare occurrence that shouldn't prevent you from engaging in BTST trading due to the minimal risk involved.

#### Delivery Process: Automated System
**How It Works:** Shares are automatically transferred to your account through your broker's power of attorney
- If you buy a Monday, the settlement happens on Wednesday (T+2), so your broker must deliver those shares to you by then
- Since the process is automated with OTP verification these days, the system is designed to minimize delivery failures
- Trust the automated system while understanding the T+2 settlement timeline for BTST trading

#### Short Delivery Scenario: Chain Reaction Effect
**What Happens:** When short delivery occurs, it creates a chain reaction affecting multiple parties in the transaction
- If your broker doesn't receive shares by Wednesday, you also face penalties for selling shares you never had
- The person who failed to deliver gets charged 10-20% penalty, and since you sold shares you never received, you also face charges
- Since this creates a "chain of bad delivery," the penalties can compound but are ultimately resolved through the exchange's penalty transfer system

#### Risk Assessment: Extremely Low Probability
**Why It's Manageable:** In my career spanning many years, it has happened only 10-15 times - extremely rare
- Since this happens so rarely, the benefits of BTST trading far outweigh the minimal penalty risk
- I would not have continued this system if I was paying penalties on every trade, so the risk is clearly manageable
- Focus on the high probability of making money versus the extremely low probability of facing penalties

#### Penalty Structure: Minimal Financial Impact
**What to Expect:** The penalties range from 10-20% of the observed share value, but the exact amount depends on market conditions
- The penalty charged to the person who failed to deliver is transferred to you, and the system resolves the difference
- Since the final difference that comes to you is very nominal, the financial impact is minimal when it does occur
- Don't worry excessively about penalty amounts since they're rare and the net impact is minimal

#### Practical Approach: Continue BTST Trading
**Key Takeaway:** Continue BTST trading while understanding the minimal risk involved
- No, you should not avoid BTST trading due to short delivery risk because the probability of making money on trades is much higher than the probability of facing penalties
- Don't worry about this rare occurrence - if it happens once a year, it shouldn't prevent you from taking profitable opportunities
- As the instructor continues BTST trading successfully, the practical evidence shows this is a manageable risk

## Q5: What should we do when trades are not hitting target or stop loss by end of day?

### Answer Summary

#### Core Principle
If a trade has **not hit either target or stop-loss by the end of the day**, you must decide whether to **carry it overnight or exit**, based on **risk management and strength of price behavior**, not hope. There is **no universal right or wrong answer** — it depends on **how the trade closed** and **how much risk you are carrying**.

#### Four Possible Trade Outcomes
Every trade will fall into **one of four scenarios**:
1. **Small gain** - Minor profit that doesn't justify overnight risk
2. **Small loss** - Minor loss that might be acceptable to close out  
3. **Big gain** - Significant profit that might warrant holding
4. **Big loss** - Significant loss that definitely needs attention

Survival in trading depends on **avoiding the 4th scenario (big loss)**. If you consistently allow big losses, **you won't survive long-term**.

#### End-of-Day Decision Framework
If you entered a stock at **₹100** and by **3:15 PM** it is around **₹101 (≈1% gain)**:

**Ask yourself:**
- Is the profit meaningful?
- Is the risk worth carrying overnight?
- Do I have a safety margin?

If the gain is **very small** and **structure is weak**, **exiting is completely correct**, especially for beginners.

#### Risk-First Mindset (Very Important)
Most traders focus on:
> "How much money can I make?"

The correct approach is:
> "How much am I risking?"

If risk is well-managed, profits will come naturally. This change in mindset **completely alters how you approach trading**.

#### When Carrying a Trade Overnight *Makes Sense*
You should consider holding a trade overnight **only if**:
- The stock shows **strong behavior**
- The **closing candle is strong** (full body, closing near the high)
- You have a **good safety margin**
- The move looks **structurally healthy**, not weak or indecisive

**Examples given:**
- Vedanta
- Amber  
- Auropharma

All were held overnight because:
- Strong, full-bodied closes
- Clear bullish behavior
- Good risk cushion

This is how **30–50% multi-day moves** happen — **not in one day**, but by **holding strong positions for several days**.

#### When NOT to Carry Overnight
Do **not** carry the trade if:
- Closing is weak or in the middle of the candle
- Price barely moved
- Behavior is unclear
- You lack confidence or safety margin

Risk of **gap-downs** is real, especially in volatile markets.

#### Judgment Improves With Experience
- New traders **will struggle to judge strength**
- This is normal
- After **50+ trades** and **3–4 months**, judgment improves significantly
- You'll naturally recognize:
  - Strong vs weak behavior
  - Which trades deserve patience

#### Learning With an Open Mind
- Old beliefs and rigid thinking slow progress
- Be open to new methods
- Reversals, once considered "too hard for beginners," started working well for traders who stayed open-minded
- One trader who earlier avoided reversals later reported **strong profits**

If something doesn't work after **6 months of honest effort**, then reassess — until then, **stay open**.

#### Final Takeaway
- End-of-day exits are **not wrong**
- Carrying overnight is justified **only for strong setups**
- Focus on **risk first, profit second**
- Survival > consistency > big gains
- Judgment improves with time and experience

## Q8: Can you show examples of base resets and how you go about the same with examples from a couple of charts?

### What is a Base Reset?

A base reset happens when a stock breaks below the lowest point of its previous base. Think of it this way - if a stock formed a base with its lowest point at ₹100, and then later drops below ₹100 to say ₹95, that's a violation of the previous base.

The key part comes next - after this violation, if the stock recovers and forms a new base structure while the moving average starts trending up again, then I consider it a reset. This means I start tracking from this new base as if it's fresh.

### Why Does This Matter?

The whole point is to help you understand if a move is fresh and young, or if it's very old and potentially tired. When you see a stock that's been going up for a long time, you might wonder - is this still a young move or is it getting old? Base reset helps answer that question.

### How I Use It in Practice

**Step 1: Identify the Previous Base**
First, I look at the previous base formation and mark its lowest point. This becomes my reference level.

**Step 2: Watch for Violation**
Then I monitor if the stock breaks below that level. But here's the important part - I don't immediately call it a reset just because it breaks the low.

**Step 3: Wait for New Formation**
I wait to see if the stock actually forms a new base structure after the violation. And crucially, the moving average should be trending up again.

**Step 4: Confirm the Reset**
Only when I see both the clear violation AND the new base formation do I consider it a proper reset.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

Sometimes you'll see stocks that just touch the previous base low but don't clearly break it - that's not a reset. Other times, a stock breaks the low but doesn't form a proper new base - that's also not a reset. You need both conditions.

The tricky part is not calling it too early. I've seen traders get excited when a stock breaks below a base low, but then the stock doesn't really form a proper new structure. You have to be patient and wait for the complete picture.

Also, don't rely only on the moving average. Sometimes the MA lags behind what's actually happening with the price. Look at the price action itself - is there really a new base forming?

### When to Use This

Base reset is more of a stock character analysis tool than something you use for daily trading decisions. It's particularly useful when you're trying to understand if a long-running move still has life in it or if it's getting tired.
